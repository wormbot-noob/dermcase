Case Overview
Non-identifying summary

A 53-year-old perimenopausal woman presents with a 2-month history of painful vulvar and perianal ulcers and fissures, with biopsy demonstrating necrotizing granulomatous inflammation and negative infectious stains, in the setting of dual-positive PR3/MPO ANCA, without oral involvement and with gastrointestinal status not yet assessed in the current episode.

Clinical Presentation
History & examination

Chief complaint

Referred due to recurrent perianal cysts and abscesses and a vaginal/vulvar ulcer, to rule out Crohn’s disease. She reports no gastrointestinal symptoms (no rectal bleeding, no change in bowel habit) at the time of evaluation.

History of present illness

Approximately from June 2025, she developed vulvar pruritus, vaginal discharge and dyspareunia, along with recurrent painful perineal and vaginal papules and cysts that drained spontaneously with yellow, blood-streaked pus. She notes pain with defecation during flares but no rectal bleeding. Bowels habits have not changed. Her husband is asymptomatic.

By late September to early October 2025, she developed painful slit-like linear vulvar ulcers and a slit-like intergluteal “knife-cut” fissure, along with multiple perianal fissures and erosions and foul yellow discharge with pruritus. Pain score was at times documented as 0/10 on examination, despite significant local discomfort historically.

A vaginal/vulvar biopsy taken on 8/10/2025 for the ulcerative lesion revealed necrotizing granulomatous inflammation. The pathology differential included infection (fungal infection, mycobacterial tuberculosis, atypical mycobacteria), autoimmune disease and foreign body reaction. Infectious stains including PAS and Ziehl–Neelsen were reported as negative, and there was no evidence of malignancy.

She was admitted under the gynecology team from 22–29 October 2025 for an infected vulvar ulcer post-biopsy, with a slit-like intergluteal fissure and multiple perianal fissures/erosions. During admission, she was afebrile, with no rectal bleeding and no change in bowel habit.

Examination

At the time of dermatology review, systemic examination was documented as unremarkable.

Local examination during and after admission showed:

  • Large vulvar ulcer at the biopsy site, with features of infection initially and later granulation tissue.
  • Persistent slit-like ulcer/fissure in the intergluteal cleft.
  • Multiple perianal fissures, nodules and erosions.
  • Significant pruritus, foul yellow discharge and pain with defecation, but no anal bleeding.

Examination at a later OB-GYN follow-up noted major improvement in the vulvar wound with granulation tissue, but symptoms had not fully resolved.

Diagnosis Findings in Favor Findings Against Further Investigation (Rule-In / Rule-Out)
Vulvar / Metastatic Crohn’s Disease
  • Knife-cut intergluteal fissure
  • Vulvar + perianal involvement
  • Non-necrotizing granulomas (matches Crohn’s pattern in literature table)
  • Chronic inflammatory course
  • No change in bowel habits
  • No diarrhea, abdominal pain, or weight loss
  • Dual PR3/MPO ANCA pattern atypical for Crohn’s
  • Focal necrosis may be ulcer-related but is unusual for classic Crohn’s granulomas
  • Colonoscopy
  • Fecal calprotectin
  • MRI pelvis (perianal disease assessment)
  • Repeat biopsy if needed
  • GI consultation
Atypical Mycobacterial Infection (NTM)
  • Ill-defined granulomas compatible with NTM
  • Ulceration and tissue necrosis can mimic inflammatory necrosis
  • Chronic nodules and fissures possible
  • AFB stains negative (though sensitivity is limited)
  • No systemic “B” symptoms documented
  • AFB culture (essential)
  • Mycobacterial PCR
  • Repeat biopsy for culture/PCR if feasible
  • TB QuantiFERON (baseline)
Foreign-Body Granulomatous Reaction
  • Polarizable foreign material present in multinucleated giant cells
  • History of chronic irritation / wound care can contribute
  • Does not explain pattern of widespread granulomas
  • Does not explain ANCA positivity
  • Does not fully explain ulcer distribution
  • No specific additional testing required
  • Considered as a secondary contributor rather than primary diagnosis
Deep Fungal Infection
(Sporothrix, Blastomyces, Histoplasma, etc.)
  • Deep fungi can cause granulomatous inflammation
  • Ulceration and chronic plaques possible
  • PAS/GMS stains negative
  • No clear travel / environmental exposure documented
  • No systemic symptoms
  • Fungal culture
  • Fungal PCR
  • Serologies if specific exposure is suspected
Sarcoidosis
  • Non-necrotizing granulomas possible in vulvar skin
  • Typical “naked” granulomas not described here
  • Ulceration is uncommon
  • No pulmonary, ocular, or systemic features documented
  • Chest X-ray or CT
  • Serum ACE (low specificity, supportive only)
Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA)
  • Dual PR3 + MPO ANCA positivity
  • Mucocutaneous ulceration possible in ANCA-associated vasculitis
  • No vasculitis on biopsy in current samples
  • Non-necrotizing granulomas not classic GPA pattern in most descriptions
  • No ENT, renal, or pulmonary involvement documented so far
  • Repeat ANCA (trend over time)
  • Serial urinalysis + creatinine monitoring
  • Chest imaging
  • ENT evaluation for subclinical disease
  • Repeat biopsy only if systemic signs develop

Comment

The differential diagnosis for granulomatous inflammation is wide and can be associated with different etiologies including but not limited to:

  • Infectious causes (fungal, mycobacterial tuberculosis, atypical mycobacteria)
  • Autoimmune diseases
  • Foreign body–type giant cell reaction

Clinical and microbiologic correlation is recommended.

Treatment vs Prognosis in Vulvar / Anogenital Crohn’s Disease
Summary of reported effectiveness across case literature

Treatment (What the literature consistently shows)

First line / frequently effective

  • Metronidazole – most consistently effective early therapy; may give rapid but partial improvement.
  • Topical / intralesional corticosteroids – symptomatic relief.
  • Systemic corticosteroids – for more severe inflammatory disease.

Second-line / for persistent or recurrent disease

  • Azathioprine (≈57% of patients in Laftah) – for persistent or recurrent disease.
  • Methotrexate – alternative immunomodulator.

Biologics

  • Anti-TNF agents (Infliximab, Adalimumab)
    • Highly effective in refractory cases.
    • Frequently required for long-term disease control.
  • Antibiotics – treat secondary infection only; do not resolve the granulomatous disease.
Prognosis
Patterns described across case reports and series
  • Chronic, relapsing, often refractory course.
  • “Vulvar-only Crohn’s” tends to be more treatment-resistant.
  • Better long-term outcomes with early immunomodulators/biologics.
  • Delayed diagnosis → worse morbidity, more aggressive disease.
  • Risk of future intestinal Crohn’s: ≈30–40% eventually develop GI involvement.
  • Secondary infection (e.g., GBS, Staph) frequently complicates the course.
  • Potential for scarring and architectural distortion in chronic disease.

Key Learning Points
From this specific case
  • Necrotizing granulomatous inflammation in vulvar/perianal biopsies requires a systematic exclusion of infection (including atypical mycobacteria and deep fungal infection) even when special stains are negative.
  • Chronic anogenital fissures and ulcers with granulomas should raise suspicion for metastatic/extraintestinal Crohn’s disease, even in patients without gastrointestinal symptoms at presentation.
  • Dual-positive PR3/MPO ANCA in the setting of necrotizing granulomas warrants evaluation for granulomatosis with polyangiitis and other ANCA-associated vasculitides.
  • Systemic steroids may provide partial symptomatic relief but should be used cautiously when infectious causes have not been fully excluded.
  • Multidisciplinary collaboration (Dermatology, Gynecology, Infectious Disease, Rheumatology, and Gastroenterology) is critical in complex vulvar ulcer cases.
